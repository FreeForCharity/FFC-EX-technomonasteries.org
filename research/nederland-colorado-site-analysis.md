## 2. Altitude & Mountain Considerations

### Health Effects at 8,200 ft

- **Reduced oxygen:** Approximately 25% less oxygen than sea level. SpO2 typically 92-95% for acclimated residents (vs 96-99% at sea level).
- **Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS):** Visitors and new residents commonly experience headaches, fatigue, shortness of breath, and insomnia for 1-3 weeks. Most people acclimate within 2-4 weeks.
- **Chronic altitude effects:**
  - Increased red blood cell production (beneficial after acclimation)
  - Slightly elevated resting heart rate
  - Dehydration is more rapid (low humidity, increased respiration)
  - Alcohol hits harder and faster
  - Sleep quality may be permanently somewhat reduced for some individuals
  - UV exposure is approximately 25-30% stronger -- sunburn risk year-round
- **Pre-existing conditions:** People with COPD, heart failure, severe anemia, or sickle cell disease should consult a physician. Sleep apnea can worsen significantly.
- **Cooking:** Water boils at approximately 196 F (91 C) -- affects cooking times, baking, and food preparation.

### Building at Altitude -- Boulder County Requirements

- **Snow load:** Boulder County requires roof designs to handle substantial snow loads. The ground snow load for Nederland area is approximately **60-80 psf** (pounds per square foot), depending on exact location and elevation. The International Building Code (IBC) as adopted by Colorado requires site-specific calculations. [VERIFY exact requirement with Boulder County Building Division]
- **Wind exposure:** Nederland is in a wind-exposed mountain area. Chinook winds (downslope winds) can exceed 100 mph in the foothills. Design wind speeds for the area may be 115-130 mph (3-second gust) per ASCE 7. [VERIFY]
- **Foundation:** Frost depth in mountain areas is typically 36-48 inches. Foundations must extend below frost line.
- **Structural considerations:**
  - Heavy timber and steel construction common for mountain residential
  - Roof pitch must be steep enough (typically 6:12 or greater) to shed snow, or engineered for snow retention
  - Ice dam prevention (adequate insulation, ventilation, or ice/water shield) is critical
  - Lightning protection recommended (high-altitude exposure)
- **Energy code:** Colorado adopted 2021 IECC with amendments. At 8,200 ft, heating degree days are very high (~7,500-8,000 HDD). Extremely well-insulated construction is essential. Passive House standard would be ideal.
- **Septic vs sewer:** Properties in town may connect to Nederland's wastewater system. Unincorporated properties typically require engineered septic systems, which must be designed for rocky mountain soils and high water tables near creeks.

### Boulder County Wildfire Mitigation Requirements

- **Defensible space:** Boulder County requires creation of defensible space zones around structures:
  - **Zone 1 (0-15 ft):** Non-combustible zone immediately around structure. No vegetation, mulch, or combustible materials.
  - **Zone 2 (15-100 ft):** Thinned and pruned vegetation, removed dead material, tree spacing.
  - **Zone 3 (100-200 ft):** Reduce fuel density, create fuel breaks.
- **Boulder County Wildfire Partners Program:** Free wildfire risk assessments for homeowners. https://www.wildfirepartners.org/ [VERIFY URL]
- **Building code requirements in WUI:**
  - Class A fire-rated roofing (metal, tile, or comp shingle -- no wood shakes)
  - Enclosed eaves and soffits (no exposed rafters)
  - Tempered or multi-pane windows
  - Non-combustible siding (fiber cement, metal, stucco) or fire-treated wood
  - Ember-resistant vents (1/8" mesh or intumescent vents)
  - Non-combustible decking within 5 ft of structure
  - Spark arrestor on chimneys
- **Boulder County has adopted portions of the International WUI Code** [VERIFY exact adoption status]

### Cost Impact of Fire-Resistant Construction

- Fire-resistant construction adds approximately **10-20%** to construction costs compared to standard building:
  - Metal or fiber cement siding vs wood: +$3-8/sq ft
  - Metal roofing vs standard comp: +$2-5/sq ft
  - Tempered glass: +$500-1,500 per opening
  - Non-combustible decking (steel frame, composite): +$15-25/sq ft vs wood
  - Ember-resistant vents: +$20-50 per vent
- **However,** some of these features (metal roofing, fiber cement siding) have lower maintenance costs long-term and are arguably superior choices regardless of fire risk.
- Total premium for a 2,000 sq ft unit: roughly **$30,000-$60,000** additional [VERIFY with local builders]

## 4. Water Rights (CRITICAL)

### Colorado Prior Appropriation Doctrine

- Colorado follows the **"first in time, first in right"** doctrine. Water rights are separate from land ownership.
- All water in Colorado is owned by the public and administered by the State. You must have a legal right to use (divert) water.
- The Colorado Division of Water Resources (DWR) and the Water Courts administer water rights.
- **This is the single most complex aspect of developing a multi-unit community in Colorado.**

### Well Permits for Multi-Unit Communities

- **Critical limitation:** Colorado well permits for domestic use are highly restrictive:
  - A standard **household well permit (exempt well)** typically serves only **one single-family dwelling, up to 3 other units (like an ADU), and irrigation of 1 acre or less.**
  - A well serving a **multi-unit residential community** (more than a few units) almost certainly requires a **non-exempt (augmented) well permit** or connection to a municipal water system.
  - Non-exempt wells require an **augmentation plan** approved by the Water Court -- this can take **2-5 years** and cost **$50,000-$200,000+** in legal and engineering fees.
- **Augmentation plans:** Required when a new well might deplete senior water rights. The developer must replace (augment) any water consumed, typically by purchasing shares in a ditch company or leasing water rights.
- **Colorado DWR contact:** 303-866-3581, https://dwr.colorado.gov/

### Town of Nederland Municipal Water

- Nederland has a **municipal water system** served by the Barker Reservoir system and treated at the town's water treatment plant.
- **Capacity:** Nederland's water system has historically been near capacity. Availability of new taps (connections) is limited and may require annexation into the town. [VERIFY current status with Town of Nederland Public Works]
- **Connection fees (tap fees):** These vary but can be substantial. For a multi-unit development, expect **$10,000-$30,000+ per unit** in water tap fees alone. [VERIFY current fee schedule]
- **Within town limits** is strongly preferred for water access. Developing outside town limits with well water for a multi-unit community is extremely difficult.
- **Town of Nederland contact:** 303-258-3266, https://www.nederlandco.org/

### Rainwater Collection

- **Legal since 2016:** Colorado House Bill 16-1005 allows residential property owners to collect up to **110 gallons** (two 55-gallon barrels) of rainwater from rooftop downspouts for outdoor use only.
- This is **trivial** compared to the water needs of a co-housing community. Rainwater collection is not a viable primary water source in Colorado.
- Additional collection beyond 110 gallons requires a water right.

### Water Rights Recommendation

**For a co-housing development in Nederland, connecting to the municipal water system is effectively mandatory.** This means:

1. The property should be within or annexable into the Town of Nederland
2. Budget for substantial tap fees
3. Engage a Colorado water rights attorney early (firms: Vranesh & Raisch in Boulder; Porzak Browning & Bushong)

## 6. Zoning & Land Use

### Jurisdiction Split

The Nederland area has two distinct jurisdictions:

1. **Town of Nederland** -- incorporated municipality with its own zoning code
2. **Unincorporated Boulder County** -- surrounding mountain area governed by Boulder County Land Use Code

Properties near Nederland may be in either jurisdiction, and the rules differ substantially.

### Town of Nederland Zoning

- Nederland has standard municipal zoning districts including residential, commercial, and mixed-use.
- **Multi-family housing** may be permitted in certain zones (R2, commercial, mixed-use). [VERIFY specific zones with town planning department]
- The town has a **comprehensive plan** and a **board of trustees** that makes land use decisions.
- **Co-housing or cluster development:** No specific co-housing zoning designation exists. A co-housing project would likely need to apply as a **Planned Unit Development (PUD)** or multi-family project.
- **Nederland Planning Department:** 303-258-3266

### Boulder County Zoning (Unincorporated)

- **Mountain residential zones** include:
  - **Forestry (F):** Very low density, 35-acre minimum lots
  - **Mountain Residential (MR):** Minimum lot sizes typically 1-10 acres depending on subdivision
  - **Rural Residential (RR):** 1-5 acre minimums
- **Multi-unit development in unincorporated Boulder County is extremely difficult.** The county has historically resisted densification of mountain areas due to:
  - Wildfire evacuation concerns (limited egress routes)
  - Water availability
  - Ecological sensitivity
  - Traffic on narrow mountain roads
- **PUD (Planned Unit Development):** Technically available as a tool for flexible development, but the county rarely approves PUDs in mountain areas. [VERIFY with county planning]
- **ADUs (Accessory Dwelling Units):** Boulder County has expanded ADU allowances in recent years. As of 2023-2024, ADUs are permitted on most residential properties, but typically limited to one ADU per lot. [VERIFY current regulations]
- **Boulder County Land Use Department:**
  - Phone: 303-441-3930
  - Address: 2045 13th Street, Boulder, CO 80302
  - Website: https://www.bouldercounty.gov/property-and-land/land-use/
  - **Pre-application conference:** Strongly recommended before submitting any development proposal. Free.

## 10. Colorado Co-Housing Legal Framework

### Colorado Common Interest Ownership Act (CCIOA)

- **Citation:** C.R.S. Title 38, Article 33.3
- CCIOA governs condominiums, cooperatives, and planned communities in Colorado.
- Any co-housing community structured as a common interest community must comply with CCIOA, including:
  - Declaration of covenants (recorded with the county)
  - Association bylaws
  - Annual financial disclosures
  - Reserve study requirements
  - Owner rights protections (meeting notice, voting, access to records)
  - Construction defect provisions (important -- has driven up condo insurance costs in CO)

### Structure Options

#### Option A: Condominium Association

- Each unit is individually owned; common areas are owned by the association
- Most common structure for co-housing in Colorado
- **Pros:** Familiar to lenders, FHA/VA financing possible, clear ownership
- **Cons:** Construction defect litigation risk (Colorado's construction defect laws have made condo development extremely expensive due to insurance costs -- this was partially reformed by SB 17-156 and subsequent legislation but remains a significant issue) [VERIFY current status of CO condo construction defect reform]
- **HOA required** under CCIOA

#### Option B: Housing Cooperative

- A cooperative corporation owns the entire property; members own shares and have a proprietary lease
- **Pros:** More communal control, avoids some construction defect issues (members can agree not to sue the co-op), potentially simpler governance
- **Cons:** Harder to finance (co-op loans are less common), may not build individual equity the same way, less familiar to Colorado lawyers and lenders
- CCIOA applies to cooperatives in Colorado

#### Option C: Land Trust / 501(c)(3) Ownership

- **Can a 501(c)(3) own and operate co-housing?** Potentially, with significant caveats:
  - The IRS requires that a 501(c)(3) operate exclusively for charitable, educational, or similar purposes. **Providing housing to members who are also the organization's insiders raises private benefit/inurement concerns.**
  - A Community Land Trust (CLT) model is the most established path: the nonprofit owns the land and leases it to residents, who own their structures. This preserves affordability.
  - **CLT examples in Colorado:** Elevation Community Land Trust (Boulder County), Thistle Community Housing [VERIFY]
  - A CLT structure could work if the mission is genuinely charitable (e.g., providing affordable housing for community benefit, not just for the founders).
  - **If the primary beneficiaries are the founders/members**, IRS may view it as a private club, not a charity. A 501(c)(3) structure requires genuine public benefit.
- **Alternative:** A 501(c)(2) (title-holding company) owned by a 501(c)(3) could hold title, but the same private benefit issues apply.
- **Recommendation:** Consult a Colorado nonprofit attorney and a tax attorney specializing in exempt organizations. Firms: Dietze & Davis (Boulder), Faegre Drinker (Denver/Boulder), Berg Hill Greenleaf Ruscitti (Boulder).

#### Option D: LLC or LLP with Operating Agreement

- A Colorado LLC could own the property with members as LLC members
- **Pros:** Flexible operating agreement can mimic co-housing governance, no CCIOA requirements if structured correctly, avoids construction defect association issues
- **Cons:** No individual unit "ownership" for tax purposes (no mortgage interest deduction per unit), members may have difficulty getting individual financing, property tax is assessed as a single parcel
- This is sometimes used for small intentional communities (5-10 people) but becomes unwieldy at scale.

### Construction Defect Concern (Colorado-Specific)

- Colorado has historically had aggressive construction defect litigation against condo associations. This drove up insurance costs and **effectively killed new condo construction in Colorado from approximately 2005-2017.**
- Reforms in 2017 (SB 17-156) required majority owner vote before an HOA can file construction defect claims.
- The market has improved but insurance for new condo construction remains expensive.
